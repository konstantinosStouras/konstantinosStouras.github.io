A fast, one-place browser for research papers across the leading journals in Operations, Management, Marketing, Information Systems, Economics and Computer Science.

I built The Lit because I wanted one place to search papers across the journals I follow, instead of jumping between publisher sites. It is a personal project, and it keeps growing.

What it covers

It is crawling all papers published in UTD24, FT50 journal lists and then includes all journals within the ABS 4*, ABS 4, and ABS 3 lists that publish papers in Operations, Supply Chain, Economics, Computer Science, Project Management and Innovation. (Crawling everything in the last lists is possible but would result in an (even) slower database of collected papers.) In this crawling effort, I have prioritized collecting information from all papers in ten core sources of my interest: Management Science, Operations Research, Marketing Science, M&SOM, Information Systems Research, Strategy Science, INFORMS Transactions on Education, Production & Operations Management, five PNAS topic sections, and the ACM EC conference (1999 to today). The database built is close to 1GB and would need a few months of slow crawling to be complete.

Key novel features

  • Key information per paper — each paper shows a link to the most updated open-access pre-print version, its citations and the papers it cited within our database. (Note that the per-paper citations are typically less than what Google Scholar would show, but that's the best we can do with free tools.) In addition, for the journals Management Science, Information Systems Research and Marketing Science we collect and display public information such as the Area the paper was published in and the Editor that accepted it. Then, e.g. for Management Science, one can search what papers got published per Area and by each Editor in separate filters too.
  • Quick views — one-click "recently added" and "open-access pre-print" filters surface the newest work and the papers you can read for free right now, so you never have to scan the whole catalog or chase a paywalled PDF.
  • Private library — sign in to star papers, add notes, build lists and tag your reading, all saved to your account, so your reading follows you across visits and devices instead of scattering across browser bookmarks.
  • Share a list — make any of your lists public and get a link you can send to anyone (colleagues, students, co-authors); they open a clean, read-only view of the papers in it, no sign-in required. Only each paper's title, authors, year and link are shared — your private notes and tags never are — and turning the list private again disables the old link.
  • Connect your ORCID — you can even create your Lit account by signing in with ORCID (“Continue with ORCID” in the sign-in window), which links your verified iD automatically; or connect it to an existing account by signing in with ORCID from your profile, or by typing your iD. Once linked — and if you opt in — you get a private “My publications” view: one click to your own papers in the database (each with its citation count) and to your author page in the Data Analytics spotlight, matched by the name your papers are actually credited with (your ORCID Published Name). Nothing about your account is made public. Ended up with two accounts (one e-mail/Google, one ORCID)? Edit profile offers a one-click merge that moves everything into the account you keep and attaches ORCID sign-in to it, so it never happens again.
  • Saved default filters — store a preferred set of journals or journal types that load automatically each time you sign in, so you land straight on the subset you care about rather than re-applying the same filters every visit. When you include Management Science, the modal also lets you narrow the default to a chosen accepting editor or area; include Information Systems Research or Marketing Science and you can pin a senior or associate editor — the same editorial filters as the main page. Prefer to see the whole catalog on entry? Save your default with nothing selected and you'll land on every journal — that overrides the built-in landing view (which otherwise opens on Management Science) for your account.
  • E-mail alerts — get an e-mail when new papers match your filters, when any new paper is added, or when the site gains a feature — with a live preview and a "Send me a test e-mail" button — so you keep up with new work without having to check back.
  • Data Analytics dashboard — a separate page of interactive charts (publication volume, co-authorship, pre-print and citation trends, an author spotlight and editorial breakdowns) that turns the raw catalog into field-level summary statistics you can read at a glance. It covers every journal in the database — the same journal list as the main browser, ABS shard catalogs included — and opens on the entire database by default, and a pre-ticked “exclude non-research items” switch keeps journal front matter, book reviews, corrigenda, announcements and indices out of the figures (the main database still shows everything; the dashboard says how many items the switch is excluding, so its paper total and the main page’s header count reconcile at a glance — untick it and the two match, up to papers added since the dashboard’s daily snapshot). Pick specific journals and every chart overlays the aggregate behaviour of the journal types they belong to (UTD24, FT50, ABS 4/4*, ABS 3) as a peer-group comparison, with toggle buttons under each chart. When you narrow to Management Science it adds accepting-editor and editorial-area filters (and senior/associate-editor filters for Information Systems Research and Marketing Science), so you can chart, say, one area's papers over time — every tile and figure on the page follows the filter you pick. The Author spotlight lets you add several authors and compare them side by side — publications per year, where they publish, and collaboration statistics (average co-authors per paper, how cited their co-authors are, citations per paper, mean disruption), each shown both under your current filters and across all their papers. A Paper comparison tab takes the individual paper as the unit of analysis: add one or more papers (search the title or paste a DOI) and compare their citations, disruptiveness (Di), team size, reference age and more side by side — with charts of citations over the papers' publication years and an impact-vs-disruption map, and each column headed by the paper's citation.
  • Team-science & disruption index — inside Data Analytics, a reconstruction of Wu, Wang & Evans (Nature, 2019) that reads off the citation network how much a paper — or an author — disrupts its field versus develops it, and how that shifts with team size, so you can gauge the character of research, not just its volume.
  • Feedback page — a dedicated page to tell me what to fix or add, where you can attach screenshots and describe the problem in as much (or as little) detail as you like. No sign-in needed; it comes straight to me. Every submission gets a unique ticket number, and if you leave your e-mail you receive a copy of your message in your inbox right away — and hear back, on the same ticket, when it's closed with what was done about it.
  • Suggest a working paper — found an unpublished working paper on SSRN, arXiv, NBER or OSF by one of the authors already in The Lit that isn't here yet? Sign in and paste its link at the top of the Feedback page. It's checked and added automatically — usually within a few minutes — whenever it fits (an author already in the database, and not already present), and you get an e-mail with the outcome. The metadata is always resolved from the link itself, so it lands identical to the rest of the catalog. And if the paper turns out to be already published in The Lit, your link is attached to it as its open-access pre-print instead — handy when the automatic finder missed it because the working-paper title had changed. (Working papers that later get published are also retired automatically and shown as the published paper's pre-print, so the same work is never listed twice.)

How to search

You can filter by journal type (UTD24, FT50, ABS 4/4*, ABS 3, or Working Papers — the listed authors' unpublished pre-prints), by journal, or by year, and search the text of authors, titles, abstracts and affiliations. A text search with no journal or type selected covers the entire database — the unpublished Working Papers archive included — so a working paper turns up in an ordinary title or author search without first picking the Working Papers type, and newly added working papers appear in Recently added papers alongside published ones; browsing by year alone, or with only the pre-print toggle, still shows published papers. For Management Science you can also filter by the accepting editor and editorial area, and for Information Systems Research and Marketing Science by senior and associate editor. Filters stack, so everything you pick is combined, and the results bar tells you what share of the journals in scope actually matched.

On every paper

Each paper carries a small badge for the most selective list its journal is on (UTD24, then FT50, then ABS 4/4*, then ABS 3). You also get one-click BibTeX, a link to a free open-access pre-print whenever I could find one (arXiv, SSRN, NBER, OSF, bioRxiv or medRxiv), and a "cited by" count that links through to the paper on Google Scholar. PNAS papers show their Significance statement too. Many papers also have a "Citing references in this catalog" toggle — with a count of how many — that lists the other papers they cite which are themselves in The Lit, with each reference's authors, and each one linking straight to that paper, so you can walk the citation graph within these journals. That list is built slowly by combining several reference sources (Crossref, OpenAlex and Semantic Scholar), so its coverage grows over time and expected to be complete for all past papers crawled here by October 2026 or so.

Where the data come from

Editors and editorial areas are shown for Management Science papers from 2011 onwards. The other sources contribute their published papers and Articles in Advance; every journal's Articles in Advance, Forthcoming and Accepted papers count toward its listing and, when sorting by newest year, appear at the top of the list — newest additions first — ahead of that year's published issues. ACM EC papers link out to an arXiv, SSRN or open-access PDF wherever one exists.

How fresh it is. Alongside a full daily rebuild, The Lit checks the eight Articles-in-Advance journals (Management Science, Operations Research, Marketing Science, M&SOM, Information Systems Research, Strategy Science, INFORMS Transactions on Education and Production & Operations Management) for freshly-published papers every 15 minutes, so a new Article in Advance appears — and joins the Recently added view — usually within minutes of Crossref indexing it, rather than waiting for the next day. Econometrica is checked the same way every 20 minutes, and its Forthcoming Papers list is followed too, so accepted papers can show up before they reach an issue. Each new paper is also enriched on arrival, so its open-access pre-print link and citation count are shown from the start. (Pre-print links and citation counts across the whole back-catalogue keep refreshing continuously in the background too.)

The Journal types filter covers the UTD24 list and the full FT50 research rank. Journals beyond the ten core sources come from this app's own dataset, which is refreshed daily, checked against the FT's list once a year, and downloaded only when you need it. The badge to the left of a title shows the single most selective list a journal belongs to, with one exception: a journal that sits on both the UTD24 and FT50 lists shows both. A broader filter still finds the paper, so an ABS 4/4* search includes every UTD24 journal. ABS ratings follow the Chartered ABS Academic Journal Guide (AJG 2024), as mirrored at journalranking.org. Beyond the FT50 and UTD24 lists I have kept ABS coverage to Operations, Supply Chain, Economics, Computer Science, Project Management and Innovation. HBR and MIT Sloan Management Review are FT50 journals and badged as such; the guide gives them no number, so they keep their last numeric grade and the ABS 3 filter still finds them.

All of the metadata is public, collected through the Crossref API, with OpenAlex, DBLP and Semantic Scholar helping fill in the EC PDF links. PNAS section labels come from pnas.org's own topic index where it has one, and are otherwise approximated from each paper's OpenAlex topic.
