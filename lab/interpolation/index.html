Interpolation • & Extrapolation on a Brownian motion

What does interpolation predict — and what happens when you extrapolate?

The blue curve is a Brownian motion (a random walk) over x ∈ [0, 100], with values y(x) ∈ [0, 1]. Pretend you can only measure it at a few places: the red points. Everywhere else the value is unknown. Linear interpolation guesses the in-between values by drawing straight lines from one red point to the next. Extrapolation continues that straight line past the outermost points, into places you never measured — where the guess and the true curve tend to drift apart. Flip “Two intervals” to split the data into two separate clusters of unequal width in different parts of the plot: two interpolation regions with a gap between them, where each side can only extrapolate toward the other. Use the buttons to reveal each layer, then drag the probe to read the predicted value at any x.

The formulae

Sample points are labelled left‑to‑right (x0, y0), (x1, y1), …, (xn, yn), where yi = y(xi).

Linear interpolation — for x between two neighbouring samples xi ≤ x ≤ xi+1
ŷ(x) = yi + yi+1 − yixi+1 − xi (x − xi)
Linear extrapolation — for x beyond the samples, continuing the nearest edge segment through (xa, ya) and (xb, yb)
ŷ(x) = yanchor + yb − yaxb − xa (x − xanchor)

Both formulae are the same idea: take the straight line through the two relevant red points and evaluate it at x. It is exact on the red points and is a straight‑line guess in between; the further x sits from the data, the less the straight line has to do with the wiggly truth.

How it works

This is a small teaching demo — there is no data collection and nothing is sent anywhere.

  • The blue curve is a fresh Brownian motion: start at y = 0.5 and add a small random step at each of the 100 positions, reflecting back whenever it would leave [0, 1]. Press New motion for a different one.
  • The red points are the values you are allowed to see. Change how many with the 3 / 5 / 8 selector — more points means the straight‑line guess hugs the truth more closely.
  • Linear interpolation (green) connects consecutive red points with straight segments. Between two points it is a weighted average of their heights.
  • Extrapolation (amber, dashed) extends the first and last segments outside the measured range. The shaded bands mark those regions — notice how the dashed line and the blue truth part ways.
  • Two intervals splits the red points into two clusters of unequal width, dropped into two arbitrary parts of the plot. Each cluster is its own interpolation region; the gap between them becomes an extrapolation zone, where the estimate comes from whichever cluster is nearer — the two clusters' guesses meet in the middle and usually disagree.
  • The probe (drag the slider or click the chart) drops a vertical marker at x. The diamond is the estimate ŷ(x), the blue dot is the truth y(x), and the tiles read off both plus the error. The formula panel shows the exact calculation for that x.
